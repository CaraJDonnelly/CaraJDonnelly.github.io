Overview

We go over the goals of Level 2, a preparatory level that anticipates Harold work in Level 3.

  • Lessons 1-3 will cover exercises for getting out of your head
  • Lessons 4-5 will cover playing background and tag-outs
  • Lesson 6 will cover interacting with the audience, including hecklers.  Covering monologues as a source of initiations or a longform.
  • Lesson 7-8 will cover the 'Commando', a simplified Armando.

Lessons 1-3 - Paul Foxcroft, Briony Redman, Paul Foxcroft

We work on three exercises:

  • Responding without pauses - not responding quickly or rushing speech, but starting to talk without anything in our heads
  • Matching big characters whilst playing different roles - understanding that this is fun to watch and need not be complicated.
  • Picking one emotion and playing it over the top

Lesson 4 - Paul Foxcroft

  • We introduce tags and sweeps - tags to advance the current thread by moving a character around in space or time, and sweeps to change thread.
  • We want a broad beginning; early on in a longform we want big variety, at least in style
  • If you think you should edit: edit.  You can always come back to an idea if it deserves it.
  • Don't swarm scenes with funny flybys (infinite waiter syndrome!) but if you realise you are swarming, swarm more.
  • Cross upstage and sweep downstage.
  • Initiating the next scene is the sweeper's responsibility.  If you can't come in for whatever reason, sweep and keep facing the wall.
  • "If all you do in a show is make one good edit, you've contributed enough."
  • If you can't follow the sweep and can't get offstage, wait for the initation and match the other improviser.
  • If everyone is onstage, those without focus have responsibility to edit.  Better to edit from outside a scene.
  • Not every scene needs a sweep.  But tags count as part of the same scene.
  • Play background.  Play extras, or objects.  Play symmetry!  It looks nicer.  You get nice *grounding* of a scene - and if you come back, the background can come back too.  By being visually interesting it allows slow stories.  Allows multiple focus, too.
  • To get a background, you need a where.  Background characters need to be careful of focus, and move slightly slower than they would normally; treat yourself to a big character and just don't make noise.  If you're being an object let your arms be the bit people will interact with.
  • Foreground characters need to see the background characters but generally not interact.
  • Tags must be quick and confident or they look awful.  Give a confident initiation, and if you are the continued element in the tag, continue your game.
  • If you're tagged out as part of a flashback, you might have to tag right back in.  This is not rude.

Lesson 6 - Paul Foxcroft

  • Heckles.  You don't need to respond to them; ask the venue to get involved if it's really bad.
  • Getting a word: British audiences like to be clever and give you hard words, and you will get the usual dross of 'gynaecologist'.
  • Keep your intro short, and don't explain structure - would you explain magic tricks before you do them?
  • Take the first acceptable word.
  • Monkey Toast form: Downstage front person gets a word, repeats it clearly to the audience, then turns to the rest of the cast, who chorus it as more.  If you don't know the definition, ask immediately.
  • Monologues: True stories; flawed weird entities with lots of little details.  They don't need a moral, a point on ending; they may not feel entertaining to you.  Share as much or as little as you like; tangents are great but don't ramble on.  Be specific; free associate.

(I realised here I could apply Jet Eveleth's idea of balance: I can take from a monologue a vocal initiation or emotive line, a World like 'changing rooms at the swimming pool', or a physical motion of the monologist to find a character; I would use whichever the piece seemed to be missing.  Months later I realised that this was pushing me always to be vocal, something I want to get away from.)

Monologues are non-performative; we become less conscious of our body language as we get caught up in our memories.

Lesson 7 - Paul Foxcroft

  • Paul quotes Jill Bernado: "Listen like a thief".  Everything is research.
  • The Commando: 'Monologue, three scenes, monologue inspired by 3 scenes, 3 scenes...'
  • This was the first time we were doing any kind of piece; none of our characters had names.  I realise I need to work on trusting my teammates; I owe them my best and that means not rushing on stage but walking on stage when I'm ready.  I take a new motto "I will give you my best".

Lesson 8 - Paul Foxcroft

  • Balance the speed of a piece: has there been a tag blitz?  Follow it with a scene with none, perhaps a slow and theatrical scene.  We want to have a smorgasboard of ideas to pick from.
  • If a character is mentioned, wait several beats to enter, so that those onstage can endow you and establish stakes.
  • When setting up chairs etc. start speaking.  There's no reason to let the energy drop.
  • "Where else could we take this character?"
