Impetus: this essay by Dan Hill on design of "smart" cities.  The essay, in short, is The Cathedral And The Bazaar applied to urban spaces; if you let people do what they want with their spaces they'll be happier and surprisingly efficient.  In the same way that the Free Software/Open Source movement will make an excellent browser but never generate Mathematica (huge project, doesn't benefit everyone, huge time requirement), crowd-sourcing urban planning will make excellent gardens and even road design, but never light railway systems (huge project, doesn't benefit everyone, huge money requirement).

The essay claimed that intersections without traffic lights or road signs are safer than those with.  Here's a video of such an intersection:

I'd like to write more, but I fear it would be waffly.  So let's rap it instead.

It's no surprise: efficiency lies in good people given their own space to rise; common sense says all streets are alive.  But now our lives are incompetence codified. Science and Enlightenment seen from the cynic's side.

I'm not saying the world's clear, not me.  Innate complexity impacts symphonically, chaotic dances of flying bees, the fall of sleet and dark city streets; these need tests before their secrets are freed.

The problem is investment in questions means questions are limited; our knowledge delimited by things we let pass; where is the dance of a bee under glass?  Making a city spring up over night leaves a question unasked:  "What would you like?".

Cars drive past and I don't see people, I see beetles, shiny and eyeless, inhuman, ill like a spine of pylons crawling their way over miles of sky.  But place those beetles in "shared spaces" with no yellow lines, parking fines, stop-go signs, their movements aren't faceless; now I'm connected cause I know the inflexions of those undirected.
