I was lucky enough to take some poetry lessons with the prizewinning Emma Jones.Â  In this poem, which I wrote for one of her classes, I try to express something abstract in an immediate way - the thing I am describing is actually Chapter One of my PhD thesis.

Gather round!
I would tell you a story.
I found, one past winter's day, a pair of binoculars
In seven pieces, sitting on a rock, on a wild hillside
With no-one around for miles. Slotting
The pieces together I
Marvelled at how they fit,
Found, as they were, on that wild hillside
Untouched by the heat of life.

These pieces, each of them
Were light
And cool to the touch.
I puzzled there for weeks as to how they might fit together.
One by one
They would slot in
And fall apart
As a confusion of waves rushing up a beach
Falls back into the sea.

Six were simple, and a seventh stuck them together
But Gordon came by, and pointed out two more
Shaped as the seventh
(He has seen more binoculars than I)
And that meant that those ought go in the middle.
I started again.
Nine pieces, three in the middle, unchanging
With six affixed and free to oscillate around them.
These wild binoculars, so beautiful,
Waver in my hands;
Rushing together like water waves filling a loch
Then melting apart as though sloughing off the burden of existence.

Did I say nine?
...I meant eleven, with two pieces found long ago by meticulous explorers:
Friedman and Schutz.

Eleven steely-silver pieces
Each pretty and well-formed
Every one a joy and a promise made
By that wild hillside.
I, lucky traveller, have heard that promise.

The rich hinterland speaks
And I listen as best as I can.
When I understand this gift,
When it has come together,
I shall heft it in my hand
Raise it to my eyes
And you and I shall watch the planets dance.
